49ers Injury Prone Again

As you know, being a football fan, in recent memory, when you think of overloaded teams in the NFL, one of the teams that comes to mind is the San Francisco 49ers. Football fans talk about the NFC West team as if they were a powerhouse destined to succeed every year. However, the team consistently falls short. While the 49ers have impressive talent, including some of the best players at their positions, their recent playoff history suggests they should not be considered Super Bowl favorites.

Since Super Bowl XLVII against the Baltimore Ravens in 2012, the 49ers have made the playoffs six times. Despite reaching the Super Bowl three times in that span, they have lost all three games, including two against the Kansas City Chiefs. In both of those Super Bowl losses, the 49ers had the lead going into the fourth quarter or halftime. Although the Chiefs were different teams in those years, it's arguable that the 49ers had the advantage on paper.

The primary reason for the 49ers' Super Bowl failures lies in their lack of clear offensive leadership. Despite having talented rosters, the team has struggled to find a quarterback who can consistently lead them to victory in big games. While Brock Purdy shows promise, he needs to take risks to become a true leader for a loaded team like the 49ers. Until then, quarterbacks like Colin Kaepernick, Jimmy Garoppolo, and Purdy may not be able to lead the team to a Super Bowl win.

For this season, I would not count on the 49ers going far in the playoffs. With star running back Christian McCaffrey sidelined due to a serious injury and star receiver Brandon Aiyuk struggling to perform at his best, the team faces significant challenges. This season could mark the beginning of the end for this era of the 49ers. NFL fans may view the team's recent history in the same way they view the Philadelphia 76ers' Joel Embiid era: a waste of potential and a team that was built to win but consistently fell short in the biggest games.

What Will Football Look Like In 10 Years?

It's no secret that American Football is the most popular sport among the US population. With over 70% of people claiming they have watched at least 1 football game yearly, the game clearly won't be going bankrupt anytime soon, right? If you are a true fan of the game, as I have been for over 20 years, then you know it has changed drastically from the early days up until now. In the past, defense ruled and was the key to success, with throwing the ball being almost unheard of. Fast forward to nowadays, and a high-powered offense is the path to victory in most cases. What may be under-discussed are the rule changes that have come along the way.

This line, specifically the one the NFL has to walk, seems to be fading yearly, and this year it has crossed over the line, affecting the play of the game. If you don't know what I'm talking about, I'm referring to the new Kick Off rules in the NFL.

This line, specifically the one the NFL has to walk, seems to be fading yearly, and this year it has crossed over the line, affecting the play of the game. If you don't know what I'm talking about, I'm referring to the new Kick Off rules in the NFL.

Yes, the first play of every game, which for most people (including myself) is one of the most exciting parts of the game, is not the same anymore. No longer will we see the likes of Devin Hester and Matthew Slater type players, because that style of play has been taken away from the game.

The new kickoff rules aren't the first and only changes, but they definitely make it seem more realistic that we may not have anywhere close to the same game of football we know now in 10 years. Between the defenses of the NFL getting more and more penalties to worry about and not being able to tackle and hit players in specific spots, and the growing concern for quality of life for players post-football, the game is becoming soft.

As I've said before, player safety is nothing to take lightly. I believe the rules are there to make the game safer for the players, and they do exactly that. On the other hand, these players know what they've signed up for. Most players of any sport start from a young age and love the sport fully for everything in it, good, bad, and ugly. Oh, and not to mention, they are being paid millions of dollars and are fulfilling a childhood dream. This leads into another topic: Shouldn't younger players of the game be more protected? And if you're playing professionally, shouldn't you be the most advanced and good enough to use the least amount of protection?

The point I'm trying to make emerges when you turn on the TV and see that the game you loved watching last year is no longer the same game you see today. Ultimately, this is what it comes down to: is player safety more important than the game? The answer to that question should always be yes. Thus, leading us to the dilemma because the safer we make this game, the less there is a game to be played.

If you ask me, I believe the game and its rules peaked around 2013-2014. This is where they established the Defenseless Player rules, which state that you can't hit a defenseless player, which should be common knowledge. Since then, we've had rule changes that make it difficult to hit the quarterback, additional defensive penalties making it harder for defenses and easier for offenses, specific areas you can kick the ball to, and specific times you can start running. I truly don't know what's next for rule changes, but I would be more okay with no kickoffs than the current new kickoff rule.

The fact of the matter is this: the game we grew up on is withering away due to society's influences, modern technology, advanced health and medical knowledge, and many other things. There comes a time when we need to look at the game for what it is and stop trying to perfect everything in it. Unpredictability in sports is part of the mystique that means injuries, amazing plays, unfortunate plays, and consequences for actions. That's also life. I wouldn't be surprised in the next 10 years if the XFL or something else emerges and makes an incredible jump into the mainstream when football ultimately becomes flag football.
